Step 3: Moving to Spheres - New Angles!

Understanding spherical triangles and their unique properties

3

üåç Welcome to Curved Space!

On a sphere, everything changes! Imagine drawing a triangle on Earth's surface using great circles (like the equator or meridians). These "straight lines" on a sphere create spherical triangles.

Key insight: We now have TWO types of angles to consider:

  • Side angles (a, b, c): The angular distances along great circles (like latitude/longitude differences)
  • Corner angles (A, B, C): The angles between great circles at their intersection points

üó∫Ô∏è Spherical Law of Cosines

cos(c) = cos(a)cos(b) + sin(a)sin(b)cos(C)

Notice: we're taking cosines of the SIDES now!

Why This Form?

On a sphere, "distances" are measured as angles from the center. A quarter of Earth's circumference corresponds to 90¬∞. The spherical law of cosines relates these angular distances in a beautiful way that reduces to our familiar flat version when the sphere becomes infinitely large (flat)!

The magic: When sides a, b, c are very small (much smaller than the sphere's radius), this formula approximates to the regular law of cosines!

The Deeper Pattern

This relationship reveals something profound about geometry:

  • Flat geometry is the limit of curved geometry as curvature ‚Üí 0
  • Euclidean trigonometry emerges naturally from spherical trigonometry
  • The cosine "correction" in both formulas has the same geometric meaning - it accounts for the angle between two sides!

This is why mathematicians say flat geometry is just a special case of curved geometry! ü§Ø
